Decode the Singularity

Every glyph on screen is one ray of light, traced backward through curved spacetime. This is a comprehensive breakdown of the physics running inside the animation. We'll go through each equation, it's significancete, and the job it does per ray, per frame.

01 Schwarzschild metric
ds² = −(1 − rs/r)c²dt² + (1 − rs/r)⁻¹dr² + r²dΩ²

The rulebook for distance and time around a non-rotating black hole. Each term says how much a step in time or space really costs once spacetime is curved. At r = rs the time term collapses and the radial term blows up — that breakdown is the event horizon. Far away, rs/r → 0 and it relaxes back into ordinary flat space.

In the sim: everything the tracer does descends from this one equation: the horizon, the photon sphere, and the shape of every bent ray all fall out of it.

02 Horizon radius
rs = 2GM / c²

The one length scale of the whole problem — double the mass, double the horizon. We measure everything in Schwarzschild units where rs = 1.

In the sim: this is why the telemetry reads clean numbers: horizon 1.00 Rs, photon sphere 1.50 Rs, shadow 2.60 Rs. Fixing rs = 1 strips G, M and c out of every equation the integrator runs.

03 Null condition: what makes light, light
ds² = 0

Anything with mass travels a path where ds² < 0. A photon always satisfies ds² = 0 — a "null geodesic". That is the definition of light.

In the sim: Impose this on every ray. It's why light bends but never slows or stops.

04 Conserved energy & angular momentum
E = (1 − rs/r) dt/dλ L = r² dφ/dλ

The metric doesn't depend on time t or angle φ, and by Noether's theorem every symmetry hands back a constant. Time-symmetry gives energy E; rotational symmetry gives angular momentum L. Both stay fixed along a ray. (λ is an affine parameter, a stand-in clock, since light has no proper time of its own.)

In the sim: conservation is what lets us bend a ray from a single stored constant instead of re-solving the full geometry every step.

05 Impact parameter: one number decides ray fate
b = L / E

How far off-centre the ray would sail past the hole if space were flat. Large b: it barely feels the hole. Small b: it's doomed to fall in. b determines a photon's destiny.

In the sim: in code this becomes h² = |r × v|², computed once at the camera. Because |v| = 1, that constant is exactly b², the same number driving the integrator.

06 Effective potential
V_eff(r) = (L² / r²)(1 − rs/r)

Feed the null condition back in and the motion collapses to a 1-D problem: a photon rolling against this "hill" built out of curvature. It can only exist where E² ≥ V_eff.

In the sim: the single peak of this hill is what carves the shadow's sharp edge — the make-or-break line between rays that escape and rays that get swallowed.

07 Photon sphere
r = 3⁄2 · rs = 1.5 rs

The top of the potential hill. Exactly here, light can orbit the hole on a perfect, but unstable circle. It's the 1.50 Rs figure in the panel.

In the sim: rays that graze this radius paint the bright photon ring, the same glowing halo the Event Horizon Telescope photographed.

08 Critical impact parameter
b_crit = 3√3 / 2 · rs ≈ 2.598 rs

Evaluate the potential at its peak and out drops the sharp divide. b < b_crit -> spiral in and vanish; b > b_crit -> escape, sometimes after looping most of the way around.

In the sim: this is why the dark shadow is bigger than the horizon, 2.6 Rs across, not 1 Rs. Gravity drags even near-miss rays inward, fattening the silhouette.

09 Geodesic acceleration: the engine
a = −1.5 · h² · r / |r|⁵

This is the entire physics engine. It drops straight out of differentiating the effective potential. There's no Newtonian −GM/r² term — light has no mass for Newton to grab. The factor 1.5 and the inverse-fifth-power pull are pure general relativity.

In the sim: applied to every ray, every step. This one tug toward the centre is what turns straight lines into the spiralling, looping geodesics you see on screen.

10 Symplectic Euler integration
v <- v + a·Δλ r <- r + v·Δλ

Update the velocity from the acceleration first, then advance the position with the new velocity. This ordering preserves the orbit's geometric structure over the long haul, so rays don't artificially drift or unwind.

In the sim: roughly 180 of these steps run per ray, per frame.

11 Adaptive step size
Δλ = clamp(0.1·|r|, 0.02, 0.6)

Curvature is violent right next to the hole and negligible far away. So the step shrinks where the bending is fierce and stretches where nothing much happens: accuracy where it matters, speed where it doesn't.

In the sim: this is what keeps 60 fps reachable. The tracer doesn't waste tiny steps out in empty space.

12 Ray fate: Coloring each glyph
|r| < 1 -> captured -> black shadow y flips ∧ r_in ≤ √(x²+z²) ≤ r_out -> disk hit -> bright glyph escapes to large r -> background -> faint lensed code

At every step each ray is tested against three outcomes: it fell through the horizon, it crossed the glowing accretion disk, or it flew off to infinity.

In the sim: this classification is literally what colours each character -> dark, bright, or dim.

13 Relativistic Doppler beaming
I ∝ 1 + β ( t̂_orbit · n̂_los )

Gas orbits the hole at a large fraction of c. Special relativity then brightens the side sweeping toward you and dims the side racing away. It's a property of the emitting gas, not of the light path.

In the sim: applied once at each disk-hit, hence one side of the disk visibly burns hotter than the other, exactly like real images.

14 Photon-ring glow
glow = exp( −(r_min − 1.5)² / 0.4 )

For every ray we track its closest approach r_min. Rays that skimmed the photon sphere (r_min ≈ 1.5) earn an extra glow.

In the sim: this paints the thin, bright photon ring hugging the shadow's edge.

≈ 180 integration steps per ray · one ray per character · recomputed every frame — general relativity rendered at 60 fps.

About Me

I've always been the kind of kid who wanted to know what happens if you push something just a little too far. What a flame does right before it tips into chaos. What ice actually does to a wing in the middle of a flight. How heat moves through a material nobody else bothered to model properly. That curiosity never really grew up. It just got better tools.

These days I build simulations of weird, specific physical phenomena, mostly because watching something almost break in a controlled way is, to me, deeply satisfying. And the same instinct shows up everywhere else in my life, only smaller. I'll spend an entire evening untangling one stubborn bug in a game I'm designing, or re-rendering a single VFX shot for the eighth time because the light still isn't sitting quite right, or staring down a chessboard fully convinced that this is the game I finally win. It never is, but I appreciate my own optimism.

I grew up gaming and never stopped. I play music when my brain needs to do something other than physics for a while. I lift weights mostly so I have a good reason to eat more. None of it is especially serious. It's all just the same quiet itch, really. Build the small weird world, push on it, and see what it does.
